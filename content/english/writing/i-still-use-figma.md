For a long time, it was simply where design happened. New ideas started there. Flows were worked out there. Conversations with developers pointed back to it. It became both the place where I explored and the place where things were supposed to be defined.

It worked, up to a point. Then it started to create friction.

Not because the tool was lacking, but because I was using it for two different kinds of work without really noticing the difference between them.

Some parts of design are about thinking. Others are about making something real. Those are not the same activity, even if they often get treated as one.

When I'm trying to understand a problem, I still prefer working visually. Putting something on a canvas, moving it around, testing relationships, seeing what holds and what breaks. That process is slow in a useful way. It forces decisions to surface. It exposes gaps. It lets ideas take shape without committing too early.

That is where tools like Figma still make sense to me. Not as a place to define the product, but as a place to think about it.

But at some point, the nature of the work changes. The questions stop being about what something could be and start becoming about how it actually behaves. States, edge cases, real data, responsiveness. The parts that don't sit neatly in static frames.

That is where I've always felt the limitations—not just in Figma, but going back through Adobe Photoshop, Sketch, and Adobe XD. The problem was never specific to one tool. It was the idea that a separate environment could fully represent something that ultimately has to live somewhere else.

At that point, continuing to design in a canvas starts to feel like working on a copy of reality rather than the thing itself.

Over time, many tools have tried to close that gap.

Design tokens, shared libraries, syncing with codebases. I've used most of it. Tools like Figma have moved a long way here. You can define tokens, sync them to a repository, and treat them as a source of truth. And to be fair, that works.

But even then, something remains unresolved.

The moment behavior becomes part of the problem—states, conditions, variability—the abstraction starts to show. You end up working around the tool instead of within it. Creating multiple versions of the same thing to simulate something that would be trivial in code. Defining structure in one place, but knowing it will ultimately have to be redefined somewhere else.

It is not a failure of a specific feature. It is a limitation of the model itself.

The tool is still representing the system. It is not the system.

And once that becomes the bottleneck, adding more layers on top does not really solve it. It just makes the representation more detailed.

So I started moving earlier into code. Not as a statement, but as a way to remove a step.

Instead of describing components, I build them. Instead of annotating behavior, I test it directly. Instead of handing something over, I share something that already runs.

The shift is subtle but changes the nature of the work. You are no longer designing representations. You are working within the same constraints as the final product.

That removes a certain kind of ambiguity. It also removes a certain kind of comfort.

Because the moment you work in code, things become less forgiving. You can't ignore states. You can't approximate behavior. You can't rely on the illusion of completeness that a static frame can give you. But in return, you get something that holds up when used, not just when viewed.

Looking back, the main issue was not the tools themselves, but how easy it is to treat design as a single activity when working in a single environment. I used the same place to explore ideas and to define outcomes, even though those require different kinds of thinking.

That distinction was never really new to me. Coming from a background in print and physical products, I was used to moving quickly from representation to reality. Sketches became prototypes. Ideas became artifacts. You learned early that something that looks resolved on a screen can fall apart the moment it exists in the real world.

Somewhere along the way, working primarily in digital products, that feedback loop became easier to postpone. The tools made it possible to stay in representation longer than I probably should have. And over time, that started to feel normal.

Moving back into code earlier is, in a way, just a return to that same principle. Getting closer to the artifact sooner. Letting the thing itself push back.

The shift for me has been less about replacing one tool with another, and more about recognizing when I am trying to understand something and when I am trying to make it work.

Figma still has a place in that. So does code. They just serve different purposes.

I didn't stop using Figma.

I just stopped using it as a proxy for reality.

The biggest shift wasn't the tools. It was understanding when I'm trying to think—and when I'm trying to build.

And those are not the same thing.
